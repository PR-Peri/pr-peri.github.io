Understanding Linear Regression: A Step-by-Step Guide

Linear regression is a fundamental statistical technique used to model the relationship between a dependent variable and one or more independent variables. It is widely used in various fields, such as economics, finance, and machine learning. In this blog post, we will dive into the world of linear regression, focusing on both simple and multiple linear regression models. We will explain how the equations are derived and provide simple mathematical examples to illustrate the concepts.

1. Simple Linear Regression

1.1 Deriving the Equation:

To derive the equation for a simple linear regression model, we assume that the relationship between \(x\) and \(y\) can be expressed as:

$$ y = m x + b $$

In terms of linear regression, y in this equation stands for the predicted value, x means the independent variable and m & b are the coefficients we need to optimize in order to fit the regression line to our data.

Calculating coefficient of the equation:

Covariance is a measure of how two random variables vary together. It quantifies the relationship between two variables and indicates the direction (positive or negative) and strength of their linear association. The covariance between two variables, \(X\) and \(Y\), is calculated as:

$$ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{n} $$

Variance of \(X\) is:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (X_i - \bar{X})^2}{n} $$

To calculate the coefficient \(m\) we will use the formula given below

$$ m = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(x, y)}{\operatorname{Var}(x)} $$

$$ b = \bar{y} - m \, \bar{x} $$

1.2. Example:

Let's consider a simple example to illustrate the concept. Suppose we have a dataset of housing prices (\(y\)) and their corresponding areas (\(x\)). We want to find the linear relationship between the area and the price. Here's a small dataset:

Area (x) Price (y)
1000 250
1500 400
2000 450
2500 500
3000 550

Using the least squares method, we can find the best-fitting line. By substituting the given values into the equation \(y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x\), we can solve for \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\). The steps are as follows:

Step 1: Calculate the Means:

To begin, we calculate the mean of the house areas and the mean of the house prices. The mean is obtained by summing up all the values and dividing by the total number of data points.

Formula:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} $$

$$ \bar{y} = \frac{\sum y}{n} $$

Calculations:

$$ \text{House Area }(x): [1000, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000] $$

$$ \text{Price }(y): [250, 400, 450, 500, 550] $$

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{1000 + 1500 + 2000 + 2500 + 3000}{5} = 2000 $$

$$ \bar{y} = \frac{250 + 400 + 450 + 500 + 550}{5} = 430 $$

Step 2: Calculate the Deviations:

Next, we calculate the deviations of each house area (\(x\)) and house price (\(y\)) from their respective means. Deviation is obtained by subtracting the mean from each value.

Formula:

$$ \text{Deviation }(x - \bar{x}) = x - \bar{x} $$

$$ \text{Deviation }(y - \bar{y}) = y - \bar{y} $$

Calculations:

$$ x - \bar{x} = [-1000, -500, 0, 500, 1000] $$

$$ y - \bar{y} = [-180, -30, 20, 70, 120] $$

Step 3: Calculate the Sum of Products and Squares:

Now, we calculate the sum of the product of deviations (\(\Sigma xy\)) and the sum of squared deviations (\(\Sigma x^2\) and \(\Sigma y^2\)).

Formula:

$$ \Sigma xy = \sum (x - \bar{x})(y - \bar{y}) $$

$$ \Sigma x^2 = \sum (x - \bar{x})^2 $$

$$ \Sigma y^2 = \sum (y - \bar{y})^2 $$

Calculations:

$$ \Sigma xy = (-1000 \times -180) + (-500 \times -30) + (0 \times 20) + (500 \times 70) + (1000 \times 120) = 350{,}000 $$

$$ \Sigma x^2 = (-1000)^2 + (-500)^2 + 0^2 + 500^2 + 1000^2 = 2{,}500{,}000 $$

$$ \Sigma y^2 = (-180)^2 + (-30)^2 + 20^2 + 70^2 + 120^2 = 53{,}000 $$

Step 4: Calculate the Slope (\(\beta_1\)):

The slope (\(\beta_1\)) of the regression line is calculated using the formula:

Formula:

$$ \beta_1 = \frac{\Sigma xy}{\Sigma x^2} $$

Calculations:

$$ \beta_1 = \frac{350{,}000}{2{,}500{,}000} = 0.14 $$

Step 5: Calculate the Intercept (\(\beta_0\)):

Next, we calculate the intercept (\(\beta_0\)) of the regression line using the formula:

Formula:

$$ \beta_0 = \bar{y} - \beta_1 \bar{x} $$

Calculations:

$$ \beta_0 = 430 - 0.14 \times 2000 = 430 - 280 = 150 $$

Step 6: Build the Regression Line:

Finally, we construct the equation of the regression line using the slope and intercept values obtained in the previous steps:

Formula:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x $$

Substituting the calculated values, we have:

$$ y = 150 + 0.14 x $$

Conclusion:

By manually calculating the slope (\(\beta_1\)) and intercept (\(\beta_0\)), we obtained the equation of the regression line: \(y = 150 + 0.14 x\). This equation represents the best-fit line that predicts house prices based on house areas. You can use this equation to make predictions for new house areas by substituting the corresponding \(x\) values into the equation. Manual calculations provide a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematics behind linear regression, allowing us to appreciate the algorithm's inner workings and make informed decisions when working with regression problems.

2. Multiple Linear Regression

2.1 Deriving the Equation:

Multiple linear regression predicts a single outcome \((y)\) using two or more predictors \((x_1, x_2, \dots)\). The goal is to find the equation of a plane (a multidimensional ‚Äúline‚Äù) that best fits the data by minimizing the squared prediction errors.

For our housing example we want:

$$\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x_1 + b_2 x_2$$

  • \(b_0\) is the intercept ‚Äì the baseline price when Area \((x_1)\) and Bedrooms \((x_2)\) are 0.
  • \(b_1\) measures how much price changes with each extra square foot, holding bedrooms constant.
  • \(b_2\) measures how much price changes with each extra bedroom, holding area constant.

Step 1 ‚Äì The Sample Data

Here‚Äôs our dataset of house prices:

House Area \((x_1)\) (sq.ft.) Bedrooms \((x_2)\) Price \((y)\) (k$)
1 1000 2 250
2 1500 3 400
3 2000 4 450
4 2500 3 500
5 3000 5 550

We want to predict Price \((y)\) using Area \((x_1)\) and Bedrooms \((x_2)\).

Step 2 ‚Äì Compute the Means

Regression works with deviations from the average, so we start by finding the mean of each variable:

\(\bar{x}_1 = \dfrac{1000 + 1500 + 2000 + 2500 + 3000}{5} = \mathbf{2000}\)
\(\bar{x}_2 = \dfrac{2 + 3 + 4 + 3 + 5}{5} = \mathbf{3.4}\)
\(\bar{y} = \dfrac{250 + 400 + 450 + 500 + 550}{5} = \mathbf{430}\)

These means represent the center of gravity of the dataset.

Step 3 ‚Äì Deviations from the Mean

To measure relationships, we calculate how far each value is from its mean. These deviations show how each observation differs from the average.

House \((x_1 - \bar{x}_1)\) \((x_2 - \bar{x}_2)\) \((y - \bar{y})\)
1 -1000 -1.4 -180
2 -500 -0.4 -30
3 0 0.6 20
4 500 -0.4 70
5 1000 1.6 120

These differences will be multiplied together to measure covariances (how variables move together).

Step 4 ‚Äì Cross-Products and Squares

Regression coefficients are ratios of covariances (movement together) to variances (movement alone). We therefore compute the following:

House \((x_1-\bar{x}_1)^2\) \((x_2-\bar{x}_2)^2\) \((x_1-\bar{x}_1)(y-\bar{y})\) \((x_2-\bar{x}_2)(y-\bar{y})\) \((x_1-\bar{x}_1)(x_2-\bar{x}_2)\)
1 1,000,000 1.96 180,000 252.0 1,400
2 250,000 0.16 15,000 12.0 200
3 0 0.36 0 12.0 0
4 250,000 0.16 35,000 -28.0 -200
5 1,000,000 2.56 120,000 192.0 1,600

Column Sums (key statistics):

\(S_{x_1 x_1} = 2,500,000\)
\(S_{x_2 x_2} = 5.2\)
\(S_{x_1 y} = 350,000\)
\(S_{x_2 y} = 440\)
\(S_{x_1 x_2} = 3,000\)
  • \(S_{x_1 x_1}\) and \(S_{x_2 x_2}\) are variances.
  • \(S_{x_1 y}\) and \(S_{x_2 y}\) are covariances with y.
  • \(S_{x_1 x_2}\) captures how Area and Bedrooms overlap.

Step 5 ‚Äì The Normal Equations

Multiple regression minimizes the squared prediction errors:

$$SSE = \sum \big(y_i - b_0 - b_1 x_{1i} - b_2 x_{2i}\big)^2$$

Taking partial derivatives w.r.t \(b_1\) and \(b_2\) and setting to zero gives the normal equations:

\(b_1 S_{x_1 x_1} + b_2 S_{x_1 x_2} = S_{x_1 y}\)
\(b_1 S_{x_1 x_2} + b_2 S_{x_2 x_2} = S_{x_2 y}\)

Plug in the numbers:

\(2{,}500{,}000 b_1 + 3{,}000 b_2 = 350{,}000\)
\(3{,}000 b_1 + 5.2 b_2 = 440\)

This is a 2√ó2 linear system.

Solve for \(b_1\) and \(b_2\)

The determinant is:

\(D = (2{,}500{,}000 \times 5.2) - (3{,}000)^2 = 13{,}000{,}000 - 9{,}000{,}000 = \mathbf{4{,}000{,}000}\)

Use Cramer‚Äôs rule:

\(b_1 = \dfrac{(350{,}000 \times 5.2) - (440 \times 3{,}000)}{4{,}000{,}000}\)
\(= \dfrac{1{,}820{,}000 - 1{,}320{,}000}{4{,}000{,}000}\)
\(= \mathbf{0.05435}\)

\(b_2 = \dfrac{(440 \times 2{,}500{,}000) - (350{,}000 \times 3{,}000)}{4{,}000{,}000}\)
\(= \dfrac{1{,}100{,}000{,}000 - 1{,}050{,}000{,}000}{4{,}000{,}000}\)
\(= \mathbf{53.26}\)

Interpretation:

  • \(b_1\): For each extra square foot of area, the price increases by about $54 if bedrooms stay constant.
  • \(b_2\): Each additional bedroom adds roughly $53,000 if area stays constant.

Step 6 ‚Äì Find the Intercept

The intercept ensures the regression plane passes through the mean:

\(b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}_1 - b_2 \bar{x}_2\)
\(b_0 = 430 - (0.05435 \times 2000) - (53.26 \times 3.4)\)
\(b_0 = 430 - 108.7 - 180.98 \approx \mathbf{140.22}\)

Step 7 ‚Äì Final Regression Equation

\(\hat{y} = 140.22 + 0.05435 x_1 + 53.26 x_2\)

This is the best-fit plane that predicts house price \((y)\) from area \((x_1)\) and number of bedrooms \((x_2)\).

Step 8 ‚Äì Prediction Example

For a 2,200 sq.ft. house with 4 bedrooms:

\(\hat{y} = 140.22 + 0.05435(2200) + 53.26(4) \approx \mathbf{472.8}\)

Predicted price ‚âà $473,000.

Key Insights

  • Cross-products like \(S_{x_1 y}\) and \(S_{x_1 x_2}\) show how variables move together.
  • The normal equations come from calculus but simplify to algebra, giving coefficients that minimize total squared error.
  • Each coefficient is a partial effect:
    • \(b_1\) measures the effect of area while holding bedrooms constant.
    • \(b_2\) measures the effect of bedrooms while holding area constant.

Conclusion

In this walkthrough, we built a multiple linear regression model completely by hand‚Äîfrom finding the means to calculating cross-products, solving the normal equations, and finally writing the prediction formula.

The final equation,

\(\hat{y} = 140.22 + 0.05435 x_1 + 53.26 x_2\)

shows that:

  • Area \((x_1)\) adds about $54 per square foot to a home‚Äôs value when the number of bedrooms is held constant.
  • Bedrooms \((x_2)\) add roughly $53,000 per room, independent of house size.

By working through each calculation, you can clearly see how every number in a regression model comes from the data not a black box. This approach not only deepens understanding of regression mechanics but also builds trust in the results when you later use tools like Python to handle larger datasets.

Takeaway: Understanding the math behind regression is like looking under the hood of a car you‚Äôll drive better (and trust your model more) when you know how the engine works.
